<h2>How to Use</h2>
<ol>
  <li>Pick a <strong>base time</strong> with the date and time picker, or click <em>Now</em> to use the current moment.</li>
  <li>Choose a <strong>source timezone</strong>. The tool defaults to your browser's local zone.</li>
  <li>Add one or more <strong>target zones</strong> by typing a city name (for example <code>tokyo</code>) or an IANA identifier (for example <code>Asia/Tokyo</code>).</li>
  <li>Each target row shows the converted local time, the UTC offset, the standard or daylight abbreviation, and a DST badge when a transition is nearby.</li>
  <li>Click <em>Share link</em> to copy a URL that reproduces the same comparison for anyone who opens it.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Why IANA Identifiers Matter</h2>
<p>
  Timezone abbreviations like <code>PST</code>, <code>CST</code>, or <code>IST</code> are ambiguous. <code>CST</code> alone can mean Central Standard Time in North America, China Standard Time, or Cuba Standard Time. The IANA timezone database removes the ambiguity by tagging every region with a continent and city — <code>America/Chicago</code>, <code>Asia/Shanghai</code>, <code>America/Havana</code> — and ships the full historical record of offsets and DST rules.
</p>
<p>
  This tool always converts through IANA identifiers. The abbreviation shown beside each row is purely for human readability; the math runs on canonical zone names.
</p>

<h2>Daylight Saving Time and the Seven-Day Window</h2>
<p>
  DST transitions matter when scheduling cross-border meetings near a spring or fall change. The tool computes the offset on the base date and again at every day in the surrounding seven days. If any nearby day reports a different offset, a <em>DST shift</em> badge surfaces with the day count, so a recipient pasting the link a week later still sees the correct conversion.
</p>
<p>
  Zones that do not observe DST (for example <code>Asia/Tokyo</code>, <code>Asia/Shanghai</code>, most of <code>Australia/Brisbane</code>) display a <em>No DST</em> badge instead.
</p>

<h2>Shareable Links</h2>
<p>
  The URL hash carries three parameters: <code>t</code> for the base time, <code>s</code> for the source zone, and <code>z</code> for a comma-separated list of target zones. Anyone opening the link reads the same parameters and rebuilds the comparison locally — no server is involved.
</p>

<h2>Privacy and Offline Use</h2>
<p>
  Every conversion happens in your browser using <code>Intl.DateTimeFormat</code>. No times, zones, or links are sent to a server. The page works offline once it has loaded; you can keep the tab open while travelling without service.
</p>
